A few days ago I was feeling pumped and wrote a quick blog post called Generation Z.

I made huge plans. I want to end this year well.

How? I'll have to make a separate blog post for that one. Perhaps later I'll edit this page to link it here.

But my overarching goal, for now is to focus on 1 thing: **web systems.**

<img src="/photos/daysofcode/2023W/3rings.jpeg">

Tackling down this giant beast, requires 3 things:

1. computation

2. infra

3. data

# Computation

This takes the lowest priority of my learning

- PL: compiler engineering, design

- paradigms: OOP, FP

- algorithms: AI, search, sort

- math: mostly finite math

# Infra

This is more on the combination of systems, infra, and devops.

- Systems: network protocols, systems programming

- platform: MQ, caching, load balancing

- Infra: cloud (AWS), provisioning (i.e. Terraform), orchestration (Docker/K8s)

- DevOps: deploy, monitor, alert

# Data

Data in my case, refers more towards the platform of data.

- data platform: RDBMS, NoSQL, DB design

- data engineering: ETL, pipelines

# Intersections

The overlapping rings

- computation ∩ data: data science, machine learning, visualization

- data ∩ infra: MLOps/AIOps, storage engine engineering, data infra

- computation ∩ infra: blockchain/crypto, information retrieval, ML systems

# Centre

Where Computation, Data, and Infra overlap...

At the center is the core of it all: Modern Web Systems Engineering.

Because the web is the best interface in terms of accessibility and as a result, the most popular interface for these technologies

- frontend: component-based view (React/Vue), state management

- backend: business logic, API design, design patterns, DDD

- computation ∩ data ∩ infra: scalable data intensive web applications

# Commit.

I was supposed to start this 100 days of code journey around the start of this month. But I got busy, went on vacation, had some trips to settle, and more. Recently I have just finished a weekend getaway to surf and join a [spartan race](https://www.spartan.com/) with my crew. LOL we didnt even get some sleep for the race that day. Personally, I was too excited to sleep. I had like 4 total hours of sleep for the 48 hours from the race and zero sleep on the 24 hours leading to the race. But once you commit to something, you do it.

<img src="/photos/daysofcode/2023W/winter.jpeg">

> left-right: Niles, JV, Jim, and to the right that's me

Despite no sleep and missing a few meals, and not preparing for the event, turns out it wasnt that bad, we immediately decided to sign up for the next race, and tackle harder challenges. I wouldnt have realised how approachable many things are until you decide to just do it. Like I didnt knew I could [climb a rope](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bRRiFN6UmI) with no training at all, I just powered through and used my grip, didnt use any technique like using my foot to do something like wrap around the rope or some other trick. My only preparation was watching the movie Gladiator the night before and having a somewhat active lifestyle.

I looked at all the achievements I've had in life, how far I've come, and they were there because I simply commited to it. We spend too much time in preparation, and far too less in doing.

Such as I see people who grind leetcode every day and they still aren't working in a company that demands acing leetcode interviews (e.g. FAANG companies), they grind leetcode to get to such companies but they end up wasting their time because they lack the will to take the next step: take action. They're not applying for those jobs or reaching out to recruiters and people.

They're in limbo. At a steady state of no progress.

Meanwhile all of my friends who joined leetcode companies, got there NOT BECAUSE of grinding everyday, but because they applied. Then once they got a call to interview, that's when they would grind hard for 1-3 months. Such companies let applicants space out interviews anyway over 3 months to give time to prep. If they fail, they try again, apply then grind once the opportunity opens. That's the way to go, trial by fire. Meanwhile those daily leetcode grinders, they havent seen actual combat and thus more bound to fail because even though they might be able to solve a leetcode hard at practice -- they do not have the experience of battle: cant manage the time in the interview, cant structure their thoughts well for the interviewer, cant whiteboard or demo their thoughts on collab/google docs, etc.

Another example I came across recently are those discord study groups. Loads of discord groups for people studying computer science and AI and compiler engineering and functional programming theory, etc. Bunch of interns/newgrads/juniors studying system design for years. Yet once you begin checking many of the members' earlier messages, you realise they've been grinding for years and they still havent applied their learnings! They havent applied to some job or built a project with it.

They remain in the status quo, perpetual grind mode, because they are not challenging the doctrine.

There's this interview of Noam Chomsky where he gets asked about what it means to be truely educated. He highlights the idea that it's about having the ability to inquire, create, and question independently, utilizing available resources effectively.

To summarize, we must develop a path of inquiry.

And because of that, I decided to commit to just 100 days of programming.

Cause it's short. It satisfies the quest to find feedback loops. Take action more. And if it fails, reflect and redo. That's our path of inquiry.

Will I be able to learn all of those things I mentioned above? Should I instead focus on learning one thing at a time? Should I design a better curriculum for myself? What should I learn first, fundamentals or go straight into X? These questions are a waste of time. Let battle experience be the teacher. Obviously I'll only get to tackle and be good at a few ones anyway.

It's kind of like with video games like [D.O.T.A.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dota) or [Tekken](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tekken), no world class player will be the best at all the heroes, but only at like half a dozen or so. And that's alright. You'll only get to figure out which one you'll be good at by taking action. I'm not going to be a good basketball player or DOTA player by watching replays. Well I could, but only to a tiny degree. The trial is always the way forward.

So do the 100 days, then attack at dawn.

Here's a quote from [Peter Thiel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Thiel) (PayPal + Palantir founder) to ponder on:

> If you go back 20 or 25 years, I wish I would have known that there was no need to wait.
>
> I went to college. I went to law school. I worked in law and banking, though not for terribly long.
>
> But not until I started PayPal did I fully realize that you don’t have to wait to start something.
>
> So if you’re planning to do something with your life, if you have a 10-year plan of how to get there, you should ask:
>
> <mark>Why can’t you do this in 6 months?</mark>
>
> Sometimes, you have to actually go through the complex 10-year trajectory. But it’s at least worth asking whether that’s the story you’re telling yourself, or whether that’s the reality.
