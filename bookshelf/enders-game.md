## Ender's Game by Orson Scott Card

<img src="/photos/books/enders-game.jpeg" height ="300">

I read this in around 2013, a few months before [the movie](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ender%27s_Game_(film)) starring Harrison Ford based on it was shown. I saw the book at a bookstore saying it's becoming a motion picture, and bought it. I dont have that book now, as when the movie hit the theatres, I gave it away to a friend from school, Arlan. After seeing the movie, a part of me wishes I hadnt, and especially now that I am more mature to understand the subtexts in the book.

Much of this book's value is in the mental dialogue with the main character, Ender.

That's something that cant be depicted on film.

Part coming of age story and part military strategy scifi, the book is in a league of its own. You spend most of the time reading Ender's thoughts. How he reacted to every challenge that came towards him from military bootcamp training to leading a fleet. How he analyzed situations and how he delegated tasks for his team. How he excused his brutal solutions and grappled with ethical dilemmas. How he worked around "office politics" and power dynamics.

Interestingly, the book is in the US Marines required reading list:

> Since the creation of the Commandant’s Professional Reading List in 1988, Ender’s Game by Orson Scott Card book has been required reading for all Marines. At the time, the book was considered a primary tool to illustrate many of the principles of Maneuver Warfare. In addition, the book presented visionary ideas about wargames, and realistic force-on-force “free-play” training.
>
> ([source](https://mca-marines.org/blog/2019/06/10/call-to-action-enders-game/))

In 2013, I was a young teenager when I first read it, and its biggest benefit was teaching me adversarial thinking. As I write this review the year is 2023, a decade since I first read it. Looking back from what memories I have of the book, I could now see the power dynamics at play that I have missed out on my first reading of it, and much more.

It's certainly a book that offers a lot, one that asks you to at least read it twice.

I think I should get a good physical copy of it again, to read it once more. It's just that good.
