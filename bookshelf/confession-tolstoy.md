## A Confession by Leo Tolstoy

<img src="https://images-na.ssl-images-amazon.com/images/S/compressed.photo.goodreads.com/books/1348237118i/26244.jpg" height ="300">

What if there is no afterlife, to what then is the purpose of life?

A secular version:

What's the purpose of life when all will come to an inevitable end? What is the point of all suffering?

This is the question posed by philosopher and writer Leo Tolstoy.

Tolstoy is more remembered as a writer for books heralded among the greatest pieces of literature produced such as [War and Peace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_and_Peace) and [Anna Karenina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anna_Karenina). His wikipedia page shows the greatness:

> a Russian writer regarded as one of the greatest authors of all time. He received nominations for the <mark>Nobel Prize in Literature every year from 1902 to 1906 and for the Nobel Peace Prize in 1901, 1902, and 1909</mark>

The book (technically it is an essay) A Confession was made during a time he went a major spiritual change in his 50s, shifting from being an atheist to Catholicism, and finally to Christian; he was excommunicated from the Catholic church for his criticisms on organized religion, he preferred to just follow the word of Christ.

It reflects Leo Tolstoy's profound spiritual crisis and transformation, embarking from a state of existential despair and questioning the meaning of life, to a profound engagement with spiritual and philosophical matters. He grappled with the fear of death and the seeming futility of life.

It's a voyage where he starts by wading through the science and philosophical arguments of his time, to eventually contemplating about suicide, and later with theological beliefs, the argument between suffering and the idea of a loving God, and more until he found spiritual alignment with the [sermon of Jesus Christ on the Mount](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sermon_on_the_Mount). From a journey starting with despair to finding inner peace. It has some insights that even those who arent Christian can get lessons from. It is actually a book recommended to me by a Muslim friend

It is thus something I recommend for those

1. On a journey towards finding their purpose

2. Those grappling with nihilism can find solace through his candid journey

3. If you want to have a starting point for introspection and evaluating life

4. A moral framework for living, as the parts of the book that foucses on the teachings of Christ is more on non-violence and love, and more universal moral teachings

5. If you want a critical look into religion. Tolstoy rejects the teachings of the Catholic church at face value, believing the Church to be corrupted

6. It presents a more diverse worldview
